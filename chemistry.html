⚡ Electromagnetic Radiation

What is EM radiation?

EM radiation is energy that travels as oscillating electric and magnetic fields, perpendicular to each other and to the direction of travel. It requires no medium — it propagates through a vacuum at the speed of light, c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m/s.

All EM radiation travels at the same speed in vacuum. What differs between types is wavelength (λ), frequency (ν), and energy (E).

🌊 Wave-Particle Duality

Light and matter exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties depending on how they are measured. This is a fundamental feature of quantum mechanics.

Evidence for wave nature

Experiment Double-Slit Experiment (Young, 1801)
When light passes through two narrow slits, it creates an interference pattern — alternating bright and dark bands. Only waves can constructively and destructively interfere. This is impossible if light were purely particles.
Experiment Diffraction
Light bends around obstacles and spreads when passing through narrow openings — a hallmark of wave behavior. X-ray diffraction off crystal lattices (used in crystallography) further confirms this.
Experiment Dispersion / Prism
White light separates into a continuous rainbow spectrum through a prism because different wavelengths bend (refract) by different amounts — consistent with wave nature.

Evidence for particle nature

Experiment Photoelectric Effect (Einstein, 1905)
Shining light on a metal ejects electrons — but only if frequency exceeds a threshold. Increasing intensity does nothing below the threshold. This proves light comes in discrete packets called photons. Each photon carries energy E = hν. Einstein received the Nobel Prize for this explanation. This definitively showed light has particle character.
Experiment Compton Scattering (1923)
X-rays scattered by electrons shift wavelength exactly as expected if a photon collides with an electron and transfers momentum (like billiard balls). Photon momentum: p = h/λ.
Experiment Blackbody Radiation (Planck, 1900)
The emission spectrum of hot objects could only be explained by assuming energy is emitted in discrete chunks: E = nhν. This launched quantum theory — energy is quantized, not continuous.
de Broglie (1924) extended duality to all matter: λ = h/mv. Electrons were confirmed to diffract in the Davisson-Germer experiment (1927) — matter truly behaves as waves.

⚛ The Bohr Model

Niels Bohr (1913) proposed electrons orbit the nucleus only in specific circular paths with fixed energies, called energy levels (n = 1, 2, 3…). Electrons cannot exist between levels.

Electron drops from n=3 → n=1, emitting a photon of specific energy

Excitation (Absorption)

An electron absorbs a photon and jumps to a higher energy level (nlow → nhigh). The photon's energy must exactly match the energy gap: Ephoton = Ehigh − Elow. This produces absorption spectra — dark lines at specific wavelengths.

Emission

An electron falls from a higher to lower level (nhigh → nlow), releasing a photon whose energy equals the gap. This produces the bright-line emission spectrum, unique to each element (like a fingerprint). Hydrogen's visible emission lines (to n=2) are the Balmer series.

Limitation: The Bohr model works beautifully for hydrogen (1 electron) but fails for multi-electron atoms. It treats electrons as simple planets in orbits — which Heisenberg and Schrödinger later showed is fundamentally wrong.

Hydrogen energy equation

Eₙ = −2.18 × 10⁻¹⁸ J × (1/n²) [n = 1, 2, 3, …]

Negative sign = energy relative to a free electron (n = ∞). Ground state (n=1) is most negative = most stable.

ΔE = Efinal − Einitial = −2.18×10⁻¹⁸ J × (1/nf² − 1/ni²)
ΔE < 0 → electron falls → photon emitted  |  ΔE > 0 → electron rises → photon absorbed

🔬 Major Contributors to the Atomic Model

Niels Bohr · 1913
Quantized Energy Levels
Proposed electrons occupy fixed circular orbits with specific energies. Electrons jump between levels by absorbing or emitting photons of exactly the right energy. Successfully explained hydrogen's line spectrum. Limitation: only works for 1-electron (hydrogen-like) atoms.
Werner Heisenberg · 1927
Uncertainty Principle
It is impossible to simultaneously know both the exact position and exact momentum of an electron: Δx · Δp ≥ h/4π. Measuring one disturbs the other. This demolished Bohr's orbit picture — electrons don't have defined trajectories. We can only speak of probability regions.
Louis de Broglie · 1924
Matter Waves
All particles with mass have an associated wavelength: λ = h/mv. Electrons diffract just like waves (confirmed by Davisson-Germer, 1927). This explains why energy levels are quantized: only specific wavelengths form stable standing waves around a nucleus.
Erwin Schrödinger · 1926
Wave Equation / Orbitals
Applied wave mechanics to the electron. Solutions (ψ) are orbitals — 3D probability maps of where an electron is likely to be found. ψ² = probability density. This is the modern quantum mechanical model. Orbitals (s, p, d, f) have specific shapes and energies.
Progression: Bohr (orbits, 1913) → de Broglie (matter waves, 1924) → Heisenberg (uncertainty, 1927) → Schrödinger (wave equations → orbitals, 1926). Each step moved from "electrons as tiny planets" to "electrons as probability clouds."

The Three Rules

📐 Aufbau Principle ("building up")
Fill orbitals from lowest energy upward. Order: 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p → 6s → 4f → 5d → 6p…
Key note: 4s fills before 3d, but when forming cations, 4s electrons are lost first.
⚖️ Hund's Rule ("bus seat rule")
When filling degenerate orbitals (same energy, e.g. three 2p), place one electron in each before doubling up. All single electrons have parallel spins (same direction). This minimizes electron-electron repulsion.
🚫 Pauli Exclusion Principle
No two electrons in an atom can have identical quantum numbers. In practice: each orbital holds at most 2 electrons with opposite spins (↑↓). A singly-occupied orbital = spin-up; when paired, second electron = spin-down.

Writing electron configurations

Long form (Fe, Z=26):

1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d⁶ → sum: 2+2+6+2+6+2+6 = 26 ✓

Fe: [Ar] 4s² 3d⁶ Ar accounts for 1s²2s²2p⁶3s²3p⁶ = 18 electrons

Electron promotions (exceptions to Aufbau)

Cr: [Ar] 4s¹ 3d⁵ (half-filled d — extra stable), NOT [Ar] 4s² 3d⁴
Cu: [Ar] 4s¹ 3d¹⁰ (fully filled d — extra stable), NOT [Ar] 4s² 3d⁹
Similar patterns in Mo, Ag, Au. Half-filled and fully filled sublevels have special stability.

Identifying errors in configurations

Aufbau violation
Wrong filling order (e.g. 3d before 4s) or skipping a sublevel. Check that lower energy sublevels are full before moving up.
Pauli violation
More than 2 electrons in one orbital, e.g. "1s³" — impossible. Max 2 per orbital.
Hund's rule violation
Pairing electrons before all orbitals in a sublevel have one, e.g. [↑↓][ ][ ] for 2p with 2 electrons instead of [↑][↑][ ].
Excited state
An electron occupies a higher sublevel than it should in the ground state, e.g. carbon shown as 1s²2s¹2p²3s¹ — the 3s electron indicates an excited state atom.

📊 The Periodic Table — Background

Mendeleev's contributions (1869)

  • Arranged elements by increasing atomic mass and noticed repeating patterns (Law of Periodicity).
  • Grouped elements with similar properties into columns.
  • Left gaps for undiscovered elements and accurately predicted their properties (e.g., eka-silicon = Germanium, eka-boron = Scandium).
  • Occasionally swapped elements when properties demanded (e.g., Te before I), later justified by atomic number (Moseley, 1913).
  • Modern table is organized by atomic number (protons), not mass.
The table's structure mirrors electron configurations. Groups 1–2 = s-block, Groups 13–18 = p-block, Transition metals = d-block, Lanthanides/Actinides = f-block. Period = highest energy level; group = number of valence electrons (for main group).

📈 Periodic Trends

Master framework for all trends

Everything is explained by two factors:

1. Effective nuclear charge (Zeff) = Z − shielding. Going across a period, Z increases but shielding stays roughly constant → Zeff increases → electrons pulled in tighter → atoms smaller, electrons harder to remove/easier to attract.

2. Number of filled energy levels. Going down a group adds a shell, increasing distance from nucleus and adding shielding from core electrons → outer electrons held more loosely.

Atomic Radius

Across a period (→)

↓ Decreases · Zeff increases (more protons, similar shielding) → stronger pull → smaller atoms

Down a group (↓)

↑ Increases · Each row adds a new shell → electrons farther from nucleus

Ionic radii & isoelectronic species

  • Cations smaller than parent atom — losing electrons reduces repulsion, Zeff per electron increases.
  • Anions larger than parent atom — gaining electrons increases repulsion, Zeff per electron decreases.
  • Isoelectronic series: more protons (higher Z) with same electrons → smaller. E.g. O²⁻ > F⁻ > Ne > Na⁺ > Mg²⁺ > Al³⁺ (all have 10 electrons).

Ionization Energy (IE₁)

Energy required to remove one electron from a gaseous neutral atom. Always positive (endothermic).

X(g) → X⁺(g) + e⁻    ΔH = IE₁ (> 0)
Across a period (→)

↑ Generally increases · Zeff ↑ → harder to remove electrons

Down a group (↓)

↓ Decreases · More shielding + greater distance → easier to remove

Successive ionization energies

IE₁ < IE₂ < IE₃ always. A dramatic jump between IEn and IEn+1 signals removal of a core electron → reveals number of valence electrons.

Na: IE₁=496, IE₂=4562 kJ/mol → giant jump after 1st → 1 valence e⁻ → Group 1 Mg: IE₁=738, IE₂=1451, IE₃=7733 → jump after 2nd → 2 valence e⁻ → Group 2

Discontinuities

Be > B: Boron (IE₁ < Be) removes a 2p electron — higher energy than Be's 2s. The 2p is both higher energy and shielded by 2s → easier to remove.

N > O: Oxygen (IE₁ < N) has a paired electron in one 2p orbital — electron-electron repulsion in the pair makes it easier to remove. Nitrogen's three unpaired 2p electrons (Hund's rule) have no such pairing repulsion.

Electron Affinity (EA)

Energy change when a gaseous atom gains one electron. More negative EA = stronger attraction for an electron.

X(g) + e⁻ → X⁻(g)    ΔH = EA (usually < 0, exothermic)
Across a period (→)

More negative (greater affinity) · Zeff ↑ → stronger attraction for added electron

Down a group (↓)

Less negative · greater distance + shielding → weaker attraction

Discontinuities

N has near-zero EA: N has half-filled 2p³ — adding an electron forces pairing (repulsion). The extra stability of the half-filled subshell makes N reluctant to accept electrons.

Noble gases have essentially no EA: Full valence shells — an added electron must go to the next higher energy level. Extremely unfavorable.

Be and Mg have low EA: Their s subshell is full — an added electron must enter a higher p orbital.

EA vs. IE

IE measures difficulty of removing an electron (always +). EA measures energy change when an electron is added (usually −). Both are most extreme (high IE, very negative EA) in the upper-right of the periodic table (excluding noble gases).

Electronegativity (EN)

Atom's ability to attract shared electrons in a covalent bond (Pauling scale; F = 4.0). Used to predict bond polarity and type (ionic vs. covalent).

Across a period (→)

↑ Increases · Zeff ↑ → pulls bonding electrons closer

Down a group (↓)

↓ Decreases · greater distance + shielding → weaker attraction to shared electrons

Noble gases: Most noble gases don't have measured EN values because they don't normally form bonds or share electrons, so the concept doesn't apply. (Some heavier noble gases like Xe can form limited compounds.)
Highest EN → F (4.0) > O (3.5) > N, Cl (3.0)
Lowest EN → Cs, Fr (~0.7)

Reactivity

How readily an element undergoes reactions — ease of losing or gaining electrons.

Most reactive metals

Lower-left: Cs, Fr, Rb. Very low IE → easily lose valence electrons. Going down Group 1: more shielding, greater distance → even easier to lose the single valence e⁻.

Most reactive nonmetals

Upper-right: F, Cl. High EN, very negative EA → eagerly gain electrons to complete valence shell. F is most reactive nonmetal (highest EN, smallest radius → new electron held very tightly).

Why F > Cl reactivity despite Cl having a more negative EA: F's tiny radius places an added electron extremely close to the nucleus with very high Zeff. Reactivity considers full thermodynamics of real reactions, not EA alone.
